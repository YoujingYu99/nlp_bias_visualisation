Linguistic Terms Explanations
  • Auxiliary Terms
    • Auxiliary verbs assist in forming tenses, moods or voices of other verbs. They usually occur between a noun or pronoun and the main verb (e.g., She will go. Most writers are female.)
  • Possessives
    • Possessives are grammatical constructions or forms that express a relationship of possession between entities. A possessive noun, for instance, shows ownership of something (e.g., women’s rights). This can also be expressed via the use of pronouns (e.g., our rights).
  • Possessors
    • A possessor is an element that possess, in contrast to a possessive, which is an element that is being possessed. An example of possessor is the sentence " Australia's John Biggins won the first gold medal for his country."
  • Premodifiers
    • Premodifiers are elements in the sentence that precede a word or phrase and add information about it. In a noun phrase, for instance, the head noun can be premodified by an adjective or noun (e.g., responsible nurse, women entrepreneurs).
  • Postmodifiers
    • Postmodifiers are elements in a sentence that follow a word or phrase and add information about it. In a noun phrase, postmodifying elements of the head noun can be adjectives, prepositional phrases or clauses (e.g., the man involved, the woman in the blue dress, women experiencing violence)
  • Subject verbs
    • A subject is the element in a sentence that performs an action (e.g., Two men robbed the store.). It usually occurs in sentence initial position, but can also be moved at the end of the sentence through the use of a ‘by’-phrase (e.g., The store was robbed by two men.).
  • Object verbs
    • A direct object is the element in a sentence to which something is done (e.g., Two men robbed the store.). Depending on the verb, an indirect object can be added to the direct object and usually indicates the recipient of the action performed (e.g., The woman gave the man a present.)
  • Intransitive verbs
    • An intransitive verb cannot or do not always have to take an object (e.g., he died; she sang).





  • Note: these definitions were strongly simplified. Further readings:

    Douglas Biber et al. 2002. Longman Student Grammar of Spoken and Written English. London: Longman.

    David Crystal. 2005. How Language Works. London: Penguin Books.

    Peter Mathews. 2003. Linguistics: A Very Short Introduction. Oxford: Oxford University Press.

    Steven Pinker. 2011. Words and Rules: The Ingredients of Language. London: HarperCollins.
